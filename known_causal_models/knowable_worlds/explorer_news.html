The real-world study — real data series, real news, and what each layer of context buys

Companion to the Knowable Worlds explorer (the dynamic study design) · design registered as §15.2 of KNOWABLE_WORLDS_DESIGN.md · PARKED — registered, not yet run
How this connects to the sandbox study (main page, §1). Both studies ask the same question — does the model notice when the world changes, and forecast from the current world rather than a remembered one? — in two settings that trade realism against certainty:
settinghow answers are checkedwhat the model is asked for
1sandbox — a small simulated system whose rules we wrote, with one causal link changing mid-recording (§1)every probability has an exactly computable right answerforecasts + structure probes on the changed edges
2this design — real data series and real news, all from after the model's trainingwhat actually happened, plus comparison forecasts computed from the dataforecasts only
Why the sandbox asks for causal graphs and this design does not (decided 2026-07-05): a causal claim is only checkable where the network is KNOWN. In the sandbox we wrote the rules, so a stated graph is exactly gradable. No ground truth exists for the causal structure behind a real series — a model could reason its way to a plausible network for anything, and we would have no authority to grade it. So every causal claim in the paper lives in the sandbox, and this arm asks one thing only: does the sandbox's forecasting behavior transfer to real data?
Why "after the model's training" matters: the change in each series — and all news about it — happened after the model stopped learning. If the model knows the world changed, it can only have learned that from what we put in the prompt. This removes the worry that the model simply remembers these events from its training data.
The hypothesis that ties the two studies together: models that cling to an outdated causal picture in the sandbox should also give outdated answers on real series that recently changed. If that holds across models, the sandbox becomes a cheap, controlled diagnostic for a failure that matters in practice. (Two further sandbox variations are registered to check that its findings are not artifacts of abstraction: give the variables realistic economic names instead of X1..X8 — does familiar vocabulary pull the model toward its prior beliefs? — make the simulated data messier, with seasonal patterns and occasional large shocks — and narrate the same table as text, one sentence per period, with nothing summarized, to test whether the model's structure failures are a reading problem or an inference problem.) A further synthetic precursor matters here: real announced interventions — Fed cuts included — are mostly rule-based responses to the state, not clean experiments, so the sandbox's exogeneity ladder (main page, v2 card) tests in advance whether a model can tell policy shocks from policy rules before anyone trusts its reading of actual policy events.

1 · Six situations (2 rows × 3 context levels) — click a cell

Every question is placed by two facts. Fact 1: did the series actually change? Decided by standard statistical tests on the numbers themselves, never by the news. Both rows are common in reality: most of the time nothing fundamentally changes, and much news — including important-sounding news — changes nothing. Fact 2: how much context does the prompt include? Three levels, each adding exactly one thing: the numbers alone → plus what the series IS ("the effective federal funds rate, monthly") → plus recent dated news. Each neighbouring comparison therefore prices one kind of knowledge, in forecast accuracy: level 1 against simple statistical baselines = pure pattern extrapolation; level 2 minus level 1 = what the model's stored knowledge of the world adds; level 3 minus level 2 = what recent information adds on top. News is gathered by the same process whether or not the series changed — otherwise its mere presence would give the answer away.

The question: the probability that the index in month 49 is above –. Old trend, never updated = a forecast from a simple model of the series fitted to months 1–30 and then left alone — how the world looked before anything changed, continued forward. Recent 12 months only = the same kind of model fitted only to the latest year. Actual probability* = the exactly right answer, computable only because we generated this demo series. A model's answer is read by where it lands among these — and because each model answers every question at all three context levels, the value of each layer (the name, then the news) is the difference between its own answers.

Two safeguards, both load-bearing. Memorization: every forecast target lies
after the model's training cutoff — otherwise level 2 measures recall, not knowledge (the model may simply
remember what the rate did). Self-recognition: a famous series may be recognizable
from its raw values alone, so after every numbers-only answer the model is asked what it thinks the series
was; questions where it names it correctly are analyzed separately, because their level 1 was never really
anonymous.




2b · A real series, walked through the procedure


The unit of the whole arm is the SERIES, not any prediction question — individual predictions
stop mattering the moment their date arrives and the answer is known. What we keep and measure
across time is the series itself and the model's answers to the same question at the three context levels. Each ForecastBench question is kept only as a
pointer to its dataset: the benchmark contributes a ready-made list of ~1,060 real data series (market
prices 447 · Wikipedia page views 338 · FRED economic series 165 · conflict event counts 60 · international
economics 53), assembled by others before this design existed — so the series cannot have been picked to
flatter our hypothesis — and covering very different kinds of change (policy shifts, crashes, viral spikes,
escalations; the differences between domains are themselves informative). The data, the questions (we write
those ourselves — the example below shows why), and the check against reality (the value the series actually
took) all come from the original public sources. Values below are monthly averages, approximate for
illustration.

an example forecast question
(we write these ourselves; each one is used once — after the date arrives the answer is known — but the series produces a new one every month)		In January 2025 we ask: "What is the probability that the rate in March is above 4.8?" The threshold 4.8 is chosen so our two comparison forecasts disagree as much as possible. Both are simple statistical fits to the same numbers the model sees: one fitted to the early data and never updated (it expects the rate to still be ≈ 5.3, so it answers 0.95), one fitted to the recent months only (it expects ≈ 4.2, so it answers 0.05). Whatever the model says tells us which world it is forecasting from. Note the purpose: this is not a
forecasting contest. The question is an instrument for reading the model's beliefs — built so that the two
possible beliefs give opposite answers, which means the model cannot answer it without revealing which belief
it holds.
(ForecastBench's own question — "will it have increased?" — gets 0.45 vs 0.12 from the same two agents; they
nearly agree, so it can't tell the worlds apart. That is why we write the questions ourselves.) The rate in
March turned out to be 4.33 — the answer was no — which matters only to confirm which of the two worlds was
actually real, so that answering from the stale one counts as a genuine error.


what the model is askedthe same question three times: with the numbers presented as an unnamed monthly series; then + "this is the effective federal funds rate"; then + the dated news above. A probability each time (after the unnamed version, it is also asked what it thinks the series was — the self-recognition check; this series is famous enough that recognition is likely, which is exactly why the check exists)


how it is scoredcompare each probability with what actually happened and with the two comparison forecasts; read the three context levels against each other — what did the name buy? what did the news buy? — and check whether the models that clung to outdated beliefs in the sandbox are the same ones answering from the stale world here




An honest wrinkle this example surfaces: for some questions
the two comparison forecasts nearly AGREE even though the series changed ("will it have increased?" — a flat world
and a falling world both answer "probably not"). Such questions cannot tell the two worlds apart, so we compute
both comparison forecasts for every candidate question and keep the ones where they disagree — the same filter the
sandbox applies when a change happens to have little visible effect at a given moment.





3 · The procedure, start to finish (registered §15.2)



1. Choose the series — the ~1,060 real data series on ForecastBench's list; any change in a series, and
everything after it, must fall after the models' training (older history is fine — knowing the old world is
exactly the situation being tested). →
2. Decide whether each series changed — standard statistical tests on the numbers; this decision, never
the news, fixes each series' row in the table above. →
3. Gather the news — an automated step collects dated news items about each series' domain; every series
gets a news set, whether or not it changed and whether or not the news mattered — judging that is the model's
job, and part of what is measured. →
4. Ask the models — the same probability question three times: numbers only (the series unnamed),
+ what the series is, + the news; after the numbers-only answer, ask what it thinks the series was (the
self-recognition check). →
5. Score — against what actually happened, against the comparison forecasts computed from the data, and
against the model's own answers at the other context levels; finally, check whether behavior in the sandbox
predicts behavior here.




4 · What already exists, and what is missing (literature check 2026-07-04; revised 2026-07-05 for the three-level design)



Forecasting with news is a well-studied topic: AutoCast (NeurIPS'22) already compares forecasts with and
without news access; Halawi et al. (NeurIPS'24) reached near-human forecasting with retrieved news; From News
to Forecast (NeurIPS'24) merges news with numeric series; EvolveCast ('25) asks models to revise forecasts as
new evidence arrives; FutureSim ('26) replays news chronologically and finds models anchor on old beliefs;
LEAF ('26) is a continuously updated benchmark. Numbers-only forecasting of raw series by LLMs is also its own
line of work (Gruver et al., NeurIPS'23). So neither "can models extrapolate numbers?" nor "does news
improve accuracy?" is new. What this design adds:



absent in the existing workwhat this design does instead


no "nothing changed" condition — benchmarks built from news are biased toward events that happen (documented in the "Pitfalls" paper)
the ★ situation: important-sounding news over unchanged numbers — believing the story over the data, measured on its own


context is all-or-nothing: numbers-only studies and context-augmented studies exist separately; the value of the series' IDENTITY alone is not isolated (a dedicated literature check on this level is running — result will be recorded in design doc §15.2)
the same model answers the same question at three levels, so what the NAME buys (stored knowledge) is priced separately from what the NEWS buys (recent information) — with post-cutoff targets and the self-recognition check keeping level 1 honestly anonymous and level 2 honestly non-memorized


ground truth is always real-world resolution
we also decide, from the numbers, whether each series changed — an independent second fact, which is what makes the six-way comparison possible


no paper pairs real-data behavior with a controlled diagnostic of the same failure
the sandbox measures slow release of an outdated world under known ground truth; this arm tests whether the same models behave the same way on real series — the transfer claim is this arm's headline
