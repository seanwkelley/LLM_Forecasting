Knowable Worlds — Study Design

Forecasting under a changing causal structure, scored against knowable probabilities (known_causal_models · paper 3 · KNOWABLE_WORLDS_DESIGN.md §§15–16)
The question. Forecasting in a world that changes demands two abilities: updating the predictive model when the regime shifts, and relating that update to the change in the underlying causal structure. This study asks whether LLMs couple these or dissociate them — when the mechanism generating a series changes, does the model update its forecasts and its stated causal beliefs, or does prediction adapt while causal understanding stays frozen? (Pilot verdict so far: dissociation — forecasts adapt slowly, the stated structure never.)
Why a built world. Real-world benchmarks score probabilities against realized 0/1 outcomes; the true probability is never knowable, and didn't know is never separable from couldn't compute. Here we write the world's equations ourselves — before and after a change we set — so the true probability p* of every question and the true causal structure at every moment are exactly computable. Predictive updating and causal-structural updating are measured separately, per item, against optimal. Everything on this page computes live: the same math the study's engine uses runs in your browser.
The design in five parts. §1 the world that changes, and the forecast questions · §2 the structure questions — asked about the edges that change, not the whole network · §3 extension: several edges change at once · §4 extension: a hidden confounder, where forecast accuracy alone requires causal reasoning · §5 controls · §6 results so far · §7 what runs next.

1 · The world that changes — and the forecast questions design §15

The data is a time series: periods evolve by lag-1 dynamics (each value is caused by the previous period's values), and at period t*=60 one edge of the generating mechanism changes. The model sees periods 1..t and forecasts t+1 — can it notice the world changed, and how fast? This demo runs the study's actual world — scenario seed 300: 8 variables, 18 cross-lag edges, exactly ONE of which changes.
What the model gets: only the raw table of periods 1..t plus the single question below. The prompt never describes the system's structure or hints that its rules might change. The grayed-out part of the plot is the future: neither the model nor any baseline sees it.

The design at a glance.



the world
12 scenarios (4 change types × 3 seeds), 8 variables, lag-1 linear dynamics with Gaussian noise. One
step ahead is Gaussian given the observed state — exact truth at every checkpoint. With lag-1 timing,
temporal precedence makes the cross-lag structure fully identifiable from the series shown — the ask is fair.


the checkpoints
t ∈ {30, 40, 55 | 62, 66, 70, 80, 95}. Three pre-change checkpoints establish that beliefs had
settled before the world breaks them — clinging to an old belief is only measurable for a belief that was
actually formed.


the items, per checkpoint
(1) a forecast on the affected variable (a change to edge i→j alters the next period of j
only); (2) a forecast on an unaffected variable — the automatic control; (3) structure questions about
specific edges — the edge that changed, plus matched comparison edges (§2) — not the whole network.


what the model is told
Nothing — detecting the change unprompted is the headline. How much the prompt reveals is a registered
ladder (told nothing → "may have changed" → "did change" → "when" → "what"); each step's difference measures
what that piece of knowledge is worth in forecast accuracy. The pilot runs the bottom rung.






The only signal there is. No row is labeled with its regime. But fit one causal model to the EARLY
data (periods 1–40) and monitor its standardized one-step-ahead forecast residuals.
Under a still-valid model these have mean |z| ≈ 0.80 (=√(2/π)); after t* the
affected variable's residuals break out of that band
while a control variable's never do. This is
residual-based change-point monitoring, and it is everything an agent gets. Slide the checkpoint to watch it
develop; switch the change type to compare footprints.








Baselines: why keep statistical agents when we wrote the equations? Because the truth and the
learnable truth differ. The equations give exact p* and the true graph; they do not say what the
finite sample reveals. The baseline agents measure that second thing, providing
attribution (every LLM error splits into model−achievable plus achievable−truth; only
the first term is a finding about the model) and detectability certification (latency
is judged against when the change was certifiably visible in these rows, not against t* itself).

probabilities out; where it sets its own evidence bar is part of what's measured.





2 · The structure questions: ask about the edges that change, not the whole network design §16.1–16.2


The structure questions ask about specific edges — the edge that changed, plus
comparison edges of matched strength — never for the whole network at once. The demo below shows why: in a
whole-network score, the one edge that matters is drowned out by the seventeen that don't.




Why the whole network is the wrong lens. The old and new worlds share 17 of their 18 edges, so any
whole-graph score is dominated by everything the two regimes have in common — F1-vs-old never collapses
after t*, because the estimate stays right about everything that didn't change. The red–green gap below is
worth at most ~1 edge in 18. Empirically, LLM whole-graph answers also sit at a floor (signed F1 ≈ 0.15, ~5 false edges per answer)
with ~9% overlap between consecutive checkpoints — there is no stable object there to track (§6). The sharp measure is the strip under the curves: the
one variable-pair whose edge differs between regimes —
red = stated as in the OLD world,
green = as in the NEW, gray = not asserted. The
red→green flip is the detection event, uncontaminated by the other 17 edges. Two strips, two ways of using the data:
roll 20 forgets old rows and flips within ~10–20 periods; accum. pools all rows and cannot flip
a sign until new rows outnumber old — accumulating is better for everything that didn't
change and fatal for the thing that did. The optimal agent accumulates within a regime, which
requires detecting the regime boundary: the skill under test.

The structure question. At every checkpoint, for each tracked pair — the edge that changed plus
3 strength-matched comparison edges (2 present in both regimes, 1 absent in both) — the model is
asked, one pair per call, for two probabilities: that the influence is present in the system's
current behavior, and that it is positive if present. Scored by Brier against the exact truth; the
read is difference-in-differences: does the answer on the changed edge move across t* while the comparison
edges stay flat? (Asking about a specific pair unavoidably hints that it is interesting — the comparison
edges get the identical hint, so the comparison nets it out.) Every change type has a predicted signature:



change at t*what a calibrated answer does on the changed edge


edge addedP(present) climbs from low to high


edge removedP(present) falls from high to low


sign flipP(present) stays high; P(positive) crosses to the other side of 0.5


weight doubledthe edge's truth never changes — but the evidence for it doubles, so
P(present) should climb toward certainty. The subtlest signature; a scenario is admitted only when the
statistician's own confidence (rolling-20 |t|, recorded with every answer) clearly jumps across t*




Two supporting uses of the same probability format: the
full-graph version (every candidate pair in one call) survives only as the belief object the
carryover experiments hand back for revision, where drift must be measured across many edges that should
stay put; and a one-snapshot diagnostic (one maximally detectable pair, the whole answer budget on
it) settles whether failures come from juggling many pairs — GPT-OSS answered no (chance even on one
pair), the reasoning model answered yes (+0.45): the floor is a limit of allocation, not of detection. A stakes variant is registered alongside: +1 per correct
asserted edge, −1 per false one, abstention allowed.

3 · Extension: several edges change at once design §16.2 add.2


The single-change design asks "did you notice THE change?" — an extension varies how
much of the network changes at t*: k ∈ {1, 3, 6} edges at once, as nested sets (the k=1 change is inside
the k=3 set, which is inside the k=6 set). This is not a difficulty dial in one direction — it splits two
skills apart.




Detection gets easier; attribution gets harder. More changed edges means more variables whose
residuals break out — impossible to miss. But locating which incoming edges changed becomes harder:
every broken monitor multiplies the candidate edges. The pilot's dissociation
(forecasts adapt, stated structure doesn't) is therefore predicted to widen with k: the "prediction"
side profits from easier detection while the "understanding" side drowns in attribution.

4 · Extension: the hidden-confounder world design §16.3


A fair objection to §1: with lag-1 timing the structure is recoverable from observation
alone, so an ideal statistician is the ceiling and causal reasoning adds nothing beyond good statistics.
This world removes that ceiling — here forecast accuracy alone requires causal reasoning, because the
best observational fit is provably wrong on a certified subset of questions.

The two X1 questions name the same value with different
  origins: one produced by the world's own mechanism, one written over it from outside. A model without
  the causal picture answers them the same and fails one; the gap between its two answers is exactly how
  much causal structure it holds. The third question blocks the cheap shortcut of discounting all
  interventions. The next card spells out the mechanism.








The mechanism, spelled out. In this world X1 has a generating equation — X1 = h·U + noise, where h is
the hidden-strength slider — and X2 has its own: X2 = h·U + c·X3 + noise. The three questions differ in what
happens to that first equation, and therefore in what the value a can say about the hidden U.



questionwhat happened in the worldwhat a says about hidden U


see X1 = a
X1's equation ran as usual and happened to output a. For a large a, by far the most common way that
happens is a large draw of U — a large noise draw alone is rarer
a is a symptom of U: the periods where X1 naturally equals a are mostly periods where U was high
(best guess E[U | X1=a] = ha/(h²+1)). That same U drives X2, so the X2 forecast must move with a


set X1 = a
X1's equation is deleted and overwritten: X1 = a, full stop. U still ran, unseen, exactly as
always — it just no longer had any say in X1's value
nothing — a was chosen by the intervener, not produced by U. The correct X2 forecast is the
unconditional one, whatever a is. This is why the middle gauge does not move


set X3 = a
X3's equation is overwritten the same way — but a now sits inside X2's own equation
(X2 = h·U + c·X3 + noise)
still nothing about U — but a is a direct cause of X2, so the forecast must use it anyway:
through the c·X3 term, not through any inference about U




The classic analogy: a barometer reading low predicts a storm;
pushing the needle low predicts nothing. The needle position is identical — it only carries information
about the storm when the atmosphere is what moved it.

Step 1 — sort the rows into two kinds. Of the 55 periods shown, 10 are starred: X1 was set from
outside in those periods, and arose from the system in the other 45. Nothing else distinguishes them — the
label is the only handle.



Step 2 — estimate each relationship from the rows where it is trustworthy. In the 45 natural rows,
X1 and X2 move together (correlation ≈ +0.42 in this window). In the 10 starred rows the co-movement is
gone (-0.03). An influence of X1 on X2 would survive the intervention; a shared unseen driver would not.
So the co-movement is not X1's doing — something unshown moves both. The X3–X2 relationship
(≈ +0.86) holds in starred and natural rows alike, so X3 plausibly does drive X2.

The discipline to notice: the same number, 6.13, produced answers
of 0.50 and 0.90. Nothing in the arithmetic differs — only how the value came
about. The exact answers come from the world's closed form; an ideal reasoner working from 55 rows
would land near them, not exactly on them, since its estimated strengths carry finite-sample error.






The evidence is in the table — an interactive look at why this is learnable. U is generated but never
shown. About a quarter of the periods are labeled interventions: X1 is set by an outside force
(marked in the series), which severs its link to U. In those rows X1 and X2 stop moving together — the
visible fingerprint of a hidden common cause.








Why this world is decisive, and where it stands. A statistical fit that pools all rows learns the
spurious X1–X2 slope and mis-forecasts the intervened rows: even the best purely statistical
forecaster is provably wrong on the set-X1 questions, and the gap can only be closed by reasoning
about the unseen cause. Every question has an
exact closed-form answer, Monte-Carlo validated (worst 2.2 SE across all five question types); on the set-X1 questions the
observational answer and the correct interventional answer differ by a certified 0.30–0.55, well above the
0.15 admission bar. The scoring is
validated end-to-end on two synthetic agents: a causal one separates see from set (gap 0.41, set-X1 error
0.00 → verdict "correct"); a confounded one collapses them (gap 0.00, error 0.41 → verdict "confounded").
The dynamic composition is registered too: the U-link can change at t* like any other edge, and a
policy-rule variant (--intervene-from t*) turns the intervention schedule itself into the

Follow-up runs (2026-07-05) — with the controls of §5 and the
specific-edge data of §2, the picture compresses to one sentence:
LLM causal beliefs are prompt-echoes — they persist only if handed back, decay uniformly
when they are, and never touch the data. Formation fails (per-edge probabilities at chance, single
edges undetected at maximal focus), retention fails without scaffolding (8% self-overlap), revision fails
even with a correct scaffold (uniform erosion; the changed edge is never corrected). The one positive: a handed
scaffold is respected (Brier .009 with it, 0.22 without) — the model can execute causal knowledge it
is given, and cannot form or revise it. The reasoning-model probe (Qwen3-235B-thinking, 15/96 items) shows
the floor and the churn surviving ~16k tokens of chain-of-thought at ~100× the cost — more conservative
(6.5 edges/answer vs ~10), not more correct.
